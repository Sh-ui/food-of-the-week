# Sous Vide Additives Guide

Food-grade chemicals for bolstering Sunday batch prep. Small amounts, big structural and color insurance over 5 days of fridge storage.

## Calcium Lactate -- texture insurance

Calcium ions cross-link with pectin in cell walls ("egg-box" structure), reinforcing vegetables against softening through cooking, storage, and weeknight reheat/wok finish. Flavor-neutral.

- **Dose:** ~1% by weight, roughly 1/4 tsp per cup of vegetables
- **How:** Dissolve in the liquid/seasoning going into the bag before sealing
- **Why sous vide helps:** Bath temps (183-185°F) activate the vegetable's own pectin methylesterase enzyme, exposing more calcium binding sites. The heat and the calcium are synergistic.
- **Use on:** Green beans, any vegetable where crisp-firm matters after days of storage

## Sodium Bicarbonate -- color lock for greens

Raises pH slightly, stabilizing chlorophyll so green vegetables stay vivid through cooking and storage instead of turning olive-drab. Chlorophyll degrades fastest in acidic conditions and at sustained heat above 180°F -- exactly where sous vide vegetables live.

- **Dose:** Tiny pinch per bag -- 1/8 tsp or less. Too much makes vegetables mushy and soapy.
- **Synergy with calcium lactate:** Bicarb softens cell walls (alkaline environment), but calcium lactate counteracts that softening. Use them together for green + firm.
- **Skip it for:** Collard greens or anything you want fully tender. The bicarb would make them too soft at long cook times. Calcium lactate alone is enough there.

## Ascorbic Acid -- anti-oxidation for storage

An antioxidant that prevents enzymatic browning, oxidative degradation, and stale flavors over multi-day fridge storage. Does not help chlorophyll (that's the bicarb's job).

- **Dose:** A pinch in the brine/jar
- **Best for:** Pickled radishes in the mason jar (keeps them from going dull/brown over the week), any cut vegetables with headspace and oxygen exposure
- **Less useful for:** Vacuum-sealed bags, where the vacuum already removes most oxygen

## Calcium Chloride -- firming agent for pickles

Same mechanism as calcium lactate but stronger and tastes bitter. Best used where a brine masks the bitterness. This is literally what commercial pickle makers use.

- **Dose:** ~0.1% in brine
- **Best for:** Pickled radishes -- firms them and the vinegar brine hides any bitterness
- **Skip it for:** Anything sealed directly with food you eat without a brine buffer. Use calcium lactate instead.

## Sunday Prep Cheat Sheet

| Component | Additive | Amount | Effect |
|-----------|----------|--------|--------|
| Green beans (both bags) | Calcium lactate + baking soda | 1/4 tsp + tiny pinch | Firm + bright green through Friday |
| Collard greens | Calcium lactate only | 1/4 tsp | Hold texture, skip bicarb (want them tender) |
| Pickled radishes | Ascorbic acid + calcium chloride | Pinch of each in brine | Crisp, no browning, all week |
