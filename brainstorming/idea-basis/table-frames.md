# Table Frames

Concrete dinner compositions organized in three tiers. Each table frame is sized for a standard 6-person night per `cai-portion-guide.md`: 3 cai + 1 soup + generous fan. The cai together are one decomposed meal, not optional sides or a buffet. Every diner accesses every dish.

Interpret all frames through the current table-service model in `fan-cai-paradigm.md`: fan bowl as personal home base, lazy susan for reach, personal serving tools for parallel access.

---

## Tier 1: Cantonese Home Table

These are grounded in real Chinese household patterns -- the 三菜一汤 (three dishes, one soup) formula that actual families in Guangdong eat on weeknights. Not restaurant food. Not banquet counts. The techniques are home-kitchen scale: no wok hei, no oil-passing, no 15-dish spread.

Sources: Chinese Cooking Demystified (CCD) "What is Cantonese Home Cooking?", Woks of Life meal planning guides, HK01 三餸一湯 series, real Chinese household dinner blogs.

### Canton Night 1: Steamed Fish, Stir-Fried Greens, Egg

**Fan**: plain white jasmine rice

**Cai 1 -- Steamed fish slices** (louder cai / anchor)
Thin-sliced white fish (tilapia, cod, or snapper) steamed with ginger, scallion, and a splash of soy sauce-sesame oil dressing poured over sizzling hot. Classic Cantonese home technique -- no wok needed, the steamer does the work. Fits on one small oval platter. CCD calls this one of the most common Cantonese home mains.

**Cai 2 -- Stir-fried baby bok choy with garlic and ginger** (vegetable cai)
500g bok choy, blanched briefly if stems are thick, then stir-fried fast with garlic, ginger, salt, and a tiny splash of soy sauce. Cantonese home cooks eat roughly 250g of greens per person -- this is the vegetable course, not a garnish. One medium serving bowl.

**Cai 3 -- Steamed egg custard** (small/soft cai)
Eggs beaten with warm water (1:1.5 ratio), steamed smooth and silky, finished with a drizzle of soy sauce and sesame oil. A small bowl shared from the center. Gentle, neutral, palate-resetting between the fish and the greens.

**Soup -- Gwuntong: milky fish head soup with luffa**
Fish head or bones pan-fried until golden, then boiled rapidly in hot water for 15 minutes until the broth goes milky white. Luffa gourd (or napa cabbage if no luffa) added in the last few minutes. Season with salt. This is a quick weekday gwuntong, not a slow-simmered weekend lengtong. CCD's exact example of an everyday Cantonese soup.

**Why this works**: Three distinct textures (silky fish, crisp greens, custard), three cooking methods (steam, stir-fry, steam), one soup that doubles as a vegetable delivery vehicle. Total protein is modest -- a few slices of fish per person, not a Western portion. The rice does the heavy lifting.

### Canton Night 2: Black Bean Pork, Garlic Choy Sum, Cucumber Salad

**Fan**: plain white jasmine rice

**Cai 1 -- Stir-fried diced pork with black bean and peppers** (louder cai / anchor)
Diced pork shoulder marinated briefly (soy, cornstarch, shaoxing wine), stir-fried with fermented black beans, garlic, and bell pepper or long green pepper. Dry-ish sauce that clings, not pools. Classic xiafan (rice-killer) -- salty, savory, drives you to the rice bowl. Small platter, 2-3 bites of meat per person.

**Cai 2 -- Blanched choy sum with oyster sauce** (vegetable cai)
Choy sum blanched in salted water with a splash of oil, drained, drizzled with oyster sauce. One of the most common Cantonese home vegetable preparations. Medium serving bowl. No wok needed.

**Cai 3 -- Smashed cucumber salad** (small/sharp/cold cai)
Cucumbers smashed with the flat of a knife, torn into pieces, dressed with soy sauce, rice vinegar, sesame oil, garlic, and a pinch of sugar. Served cold. Small bowl. Sharp, crunchy, appetite-opening. Liangban (cold-dressed) dishes are standard Chinese home table components.

**Soup -- Century egg and napa cabbage gwuntong**
Century egg chunks fried briefly until blistered, then boiled with napa cabbage in water for a few minutes. CCD's laziest gwuntong example -- flavor from the century egg itself, sweetness from the cabbage. Season with salt. Done in under 10 minutes.

**Why this works**: The pork is the only protein and it's modest -- the black bean sauce is doing the work, not a pile of meat. The choy sum is generous. The cucumber is small and vivid. Three temperatures on the table (hot, hot, cold). All rice-forward.

### Canton Night 3: Braised Tofu with Minced Pork, Garlic Lettuce, Soy Egg

**Fan**: plain white jasmine rice

**Cai 1 -- Braised tofu with minced pork** (louder cai / anchor)
Silken or medium-firm tofu braised in a shallow sauce with minced pork, garlic, soy sauce, a touch of sugar, and scallions. The pork is a flavoring agent, not the star -- maybe 100-150g of ground pork across the whole dish for 6 people. The tofu provides the body. One medium shallow bowl.

**Cai 2 -- Stir-fried lettuce with garlic** (vegetable cai)
Iceberg or romaine lettuce stir-fried fast over high heat with garlic, salt, and a splash of oyster sauce. A standard Cantonese vegetable preparation -- unusual to Western eyes (cooked lettuce?) but common in Guangdong homes. The lettuce stays slightly crisp. Medium serving bowl.

**Cai 3 -- Soy-marinated eggs** (small/cold cai)
Soft-boiled eggs marinated in soy sauce, mirin (or sugar + soy), and a little star anise. Batch-prepped, served halved at room temperature. 1 egg per person. Small plate.

**Soup -- Fried egg and tomato gwuntong**
Eggs fried in a little oil, then boiled rapidly with hot water until milky. Tomato wedges added and simmered until softened. Season with salt. Quick, satisfying, slightly sweet from the tomato. Under 15 minutes.

**Why this works**: The protein budget is tiny -- ground pork as a flavoring, eggs as a cold accent. The tofu braise is the most substantial dish but it's not a meat bomb. Lettuce as a cooked vegetable is a Cantonese home move that reads "we know what we're doing." Everything is gentle, clean, rice-forward.

### Canton Night 4: Steamed Pork Patty, Dry-Fried Green Beans, Pickled Radish

**Fan**: plain white jasmine rice

**Cai 1 -- Steamed pork patty with salted fish or preserved vegetable** (louder cai / anchor)
Ground pork mixed with a small amount of chopped salted fish (haamjyu) or preserved mustard greens (meicai), formed into a flat patty, and steamed over the rice in the rice cooker. The salt from the preserved element seasons the pork aggressively -- a few bites per person drives a lot of rice. Classic Cantonese home technique where the rice cooker does double duty.

**Cai 2 -- Dry-fried green beans** (vegetable cai)
Green beans fried in a little oil over high heat until blistered and slightly charred, then tossed with garlic, dried shrimp (optional), and soy sauce. Dry, intense, chewy. A natural rice-killer. Medium serving bowl.

**Cai 3 -- Quick pickled daikon** (small/sharp/cold cai)
Daikon sliced thin, salted briefly to draw out water, dressed with rice vinegar, sugar, and a few slices of fresh chili. Served cold. Small bowl. Crunchy, sharp, cleansing.

**Soup -- Dried shrimp and wintermelon gwuntong**
Dried shrimp simmered in water for 10 minutes to build a base, wintermelon chunks added and cooked until translucent and sweet. Season with salt. Light, clean, melon-sweet.

**Why this works**: The steamed-over-rice technique is peak Cantonese home efficiency -- one appliance, two jobs. The pork patty is assertive enough to anchor the table without being a large cut. Green beans bring texture contrast. Pickled daikon resets everything. The soup is practically free to make.

## Tier 2: Global Shared-Plate Traditions

These are adapted from cultures that already eat in the same structural pattern as fan/cai -- shared dishes around a communal staple. The research confirms these are not exotic novelties: they are how most of the world's families actually eat dinner. Each is resized to the cai-portion-guide's 3-cai + soup + generous fan formula for 6 people.

### Japanese Night (Ichiju Sansai)

The Japanese home formula is ichiju sansai: one soup, three dishes (one main protein, two vegetable sides), with rice as the unquestioned center. Portions of the two sides are together about half the volume of the main. Pickles and rice are assumed, not counted.

Source: Just One Cookbook ichiju sansai guide, Wikipedia ichiju-sansai, Sushi Man ichiju sansai article.

**Fan**: plain white Japanese short-grain rice

**Cai 1 -- Teriyaki salmon** (louder cai / anchor)
Salmon fillets pan-seared and glazed with soy-mirin-sake-sugar reduction. One fillet shared and broken into portions, not individual steaks. The glaze is concentrated -- a few bites per person with rice. Small oval platter.

**Cai 2 -- Spinach gomaae** (vegetable cai)
Blanched spinach squeezed dry, dressed with ground sesame, soy sauce, mirin, and a pinch of sugar. Served at room temperature. Small bowl. Standard Japanese home side -- made in 5 minutes.

**Cai 3 -- Quick pickled cucumber (asazuke)** (small/sharp/cold cai)
Cucumber sliced, salted, and lightly pressed for 20 minutes. Dressed with rice vinegar, sesame, and a touch of soy. Small dish. The palate cleanser between bites of salmon and spinach.

**Soup -- Miso soup with tofu and wakame**
Dashi base (instant is fine for weeknight), white miso whisked in, silken tofu cubes and rehydrated wakame floated. Under 10 minutes. This is the soup that exists at virtually every Japanese home meal.

**Why this works**: The ichiju sansai formula maps almost perfectly onto the cai-portion-guide. The salmon is the one protein and it's shared, not individually portioned. Spinach gomaae batch-preps easily (Sunday component). Asazuke takes 20 minutes of passive time. The rice is short-grain and slightly sticky, which changes the bowl dynamic -- it holds together for chopstick pickup or fork scooping equally well.

### Korean Night (Bap + Guk + Banchan)

Korean home meals center on bap (rice), guk or jjigae (soup or stew), kimchi (always present, not counted as banchan), and 2-4 banchan. For weeknight purposes: rice + one soup/stew + 3 banchan + kimchi. Many banchan are batch-prepped and pulled from the fridge -- exactly the Sunday prep model.

Source: Maangchi Korean table setting blog, Kimchimari meal planning guide, Taste Korean Food banchan guide.

**Fan**: plain white rice (or japgokbap for variety)

**Cai 1 -- Kimchi jjigae** (louder cai / anchor, doubles as soup)
Kimchi stew with pork belly slices, tofu, scallions, and gochugaru. Served bubbling. This is one of the most common Korean weeknight meals -- the stew IS the protein and the soup combined. Shared from a medium pot in the center.

**Cai 2 -- Sigeumchi namul (seasoned spinach)** (vegetable banchan)
Blanched spinach dressed with sesame oil, garlic, soy sauce, sesame seeds. Served at room temperature. Small bowl. A banchan that lasts 3-4 days in the fridge -- batch-prep natural.

**Cai 3 -- Myeolchi bokkeum (stir-fried anchovies)** (small/savory banchan)
Tiny dried anchovies stir-fried with soy sauce, rice syrup (or honey), sesame oil, and sesame seeds. Sweet-salty, crunchy, intensely xiafan. Small dish. Keeps for a week+ in the fridge -- the ultimate make-ahead banchan.

**Kimchi**: napa cabbage kimchi, always on the table, not counted as a cai because it's infrastructure.

**Soup note**: the jjigae IS the soup on this night. No separate gwuntong needed.

**Why this works**: Korean home cooking is already the world's best proof that batch-prepped small dishes + rice + soup is a sustainable daily model. The banchan rotate from the fridge (make 2-3 on Sunday, pull different ones each night). Kimchi jjigae is a one-pot anchor that provides protein, vegetable, and soup in a single vessel. This is as close to the fan/cai ideal as any non-Chinese cuisine gets.

### Ethiopian Night (Injera + Wats)

Ethiopian home meals center on injera (spongy teff flatbread) as the fan, with multiple wats (stews) spooned onto a shared platter. The fan is literally the plate and the utensil. For weeknight purposes: injera + 3-4 wats. Ethiopian cuisine has the world's most developed vegetarian shared-plate tradition due to Orthodox fasting practices.

Source: Flavor365 Ethiopian cuisine guides, Tooting Family Kitchen Ethiopian family meal, Kitchen Frau Ethiopian feast.

**Fan**: injera (teff flatbread -- buy pre-made or use a quick-ferment recipe with teff + wheat flour)

**Cai 1 -- Misir wat (spicy red lentil stew)** (louder cai / anchor)
Red lentils simmered in a base of slow-cooked onions, berbere spice, garlic, ginger, and niter kibbeh (spiced butter) or olive oil. Rich, earthy, deeply spiced. The lentils provide protein and body. This is Ethiopia's everyday staple stew. Spooned onto the shared injera platter.

**Cai 2 -- Gomen (braised collard greens)** (vegetable cai)
Collard greens (or kale) braised gently with garlic, ginger, and a little oil. Mild, clean, green. The counterbalance to the spicy misir wat. Spooned alongside.

**Cai 3 -- Timatim salata (tomato salad)** (small/sharp/fresh cai)
Diced tomatoes, onion, jalapeño or green chili, dressed with lemon juice and oil. Fresh, acidic, palate-cleansing. Spooned into a gap on the platter.

**Soup note**: Ethiopian meals don't traditionally include a separate soup -- the wats themselves are soupy/stewy and the injera absorbs liquid. Skip the separate soup on this night. If you want a fourth element, add kik alicha (mild yellow split pea stew) or shiro wat (chickpea flour stew) for a fuller beyaynetu-style spread.

**Why this works**: The injera is simultaneously fan, plate, and utensil -- the most radically fan-centric model in the world. The wats are all batch-preppable (misir wat and gomen both freeze and reheat perfectly). The protein is entirely plant-based (lentils, chickpeas) which stretches the budget. Everyone tears injera and scoops -- the communal mechanic is built into the food itself.

### Indian Night (Thali Logic)

A typical Indian weeknight thali is rice + dal + one sabzi + roti + raita/pickle. The fan can be rice, roti, or both. The dal provides protein. The sabzi provides vegetable. Condiments (pickle, raita, papad) round it out. This is the most stripped-back of the global models -- often just 3-4 components for a complete meal.

Source: RecipesHindi.in "What is a Normal Indian Dish?", Buttered Veg simple Indian dinner guide, Enhance Your Palate family thali, Prema's Culinary North Indian thali.

**Fan**: basmati rice + roti/chapati (both on the table)

**Cai 1 -- Toor dal tadka** (louder cai / protein anchor)
Split pigeon peas pressure-cooked until soft, finished with a tadka (tempered spices: ghee, cumin seeds, mustard seeds, dried red chili, garlic, hing/asafoetida). Poured over or eaten alongside rice. The dal is to Indian thali what rice is to Chinese dinner: non-negotiable infrastructure. One medium bowl.

**Cai 2 -- Aloo gobi (potato-cauliflower dry sabzi)** (vegetable cai)
Potatoes and cauliflower stir-fried with cumin, turmeric, coriander, chili powder, and salt until golden and slightly charred. Dry preparation -- no gravy. Eaten with roti or alongside rice. Medium serving bowl.

**Cai 3 -- Cucumber raita** (small/cool/fresh cai)
Yogurt with grated cucumber, cumin, salt, and a pinch of chili powder. Cooling, creamy, counterbalances the spice. Small bowl.

**Soup note**: Indian weeknight meals don't always include a separate soup. The dal itself is liquid enough to serve the soup function. If you want a true soup, rasam (thin tamarind-tomato-pepper broth) is the traditional South Indian addition and takes 15 minutes.

**Condiments on the table**: lemon pickle (achaar), papad (crisped on the stove), sliced onion with lemon.

**Why this works**: The Indian thali is the most economical shared-plate model -- dal is cheaper than any protein, roti is flour and water, the sabzi uses whatever seasonal vegetable is cheapest. The condiments (pickle, raita, papad) provide sharp flavor variation for almost no cost. The pressure cooker does the heavy lifting, which parallels the sous-vide-as-batch-component logic.

### Middle Eastern Night (Mezze + Rice)

Middle Eastern family dinners often center on a rice pilaf or bread with multiple small plates. True mezze is more appetizer-course in restaurant settings, but home cooking in Lebanon, Syria, and Palestine treats a spread of dips, salads, and one warm main as a complete dinner. The fan is rice pilaf or warm flatbread.

Source: Omayah Cooks Syrian mezze guide, The Lemon Bowl Lebanese recipes, The Petit Gourmet Lebanese spread, Independent Lebanese home cooking feature.

**Fan**: Lebanese rice pilaf with vermicelli (rice toasted with broken vermicelli in butter, then simmered)

**Cai 1 -- Bazella b'lahme (pea and meat stew)** (louder cai / anchor)
Lamb or beef cubes braised in a tomato-coriander sauce with peas and carrots. A classic Lebanese tabkha (one-pot home stew) served spooned alongside or over the rice pilaf. One medium bowl. The protein is modest -- the tomato sauce and peas do as much work as the meat.

**Cai 2 -- Fattoush salad** (vegetable/fresh cai)
Romaine, tomato, cucumber, radish, mint, and parsley tossed with sumac dressing and crispy pita chips. Bright, crunchy, acidic. A large shared bowl.

**Cai 3 -- Hummus** (small/rich/dip cai)
Smooth chickpea-tahini dip finished with olive oil and paprika. Served with warm pita on the side. Small shallow bowl. The protein-and-fat component that rounds the table without adding meat.

**Soup note**: skip the separate soup on this night. The stew is brothy enough. If you want a soup, Arabic lentil soup (shorbat adas) is the natural weeknight addition.

**Why this works**: The rice pilaf is a proper fan -- neutral, aromatic, plentiful. The stew is a real home dish, not a restaurant showpiece. The fattoush provides volume and freshness. The hummus is batch-preppable (3+ days in the fridge). Warm pita on the side gives a second fan option without competing with the rice. The whole table can go down for under $15 for 6 people.

## Tier 3: Bespoke Fusion Nights

These are the nights that could actually show up on a future FOOD-OF-THE-WEEK. They use the sous vide to batch-prep cai components on Sunday, the wok to throw together 1-3 quick dishes in sequence, the rice cooker as fan infrastructure, and the lazy susan as the service platform. Each one has an anchor, is cuisine-fluid, and follows every principle in `fan-cai-paradigm.md` and `cooking-identity.md`.

The Cantonese home table is the structural backbone. The global traditions provide flavor vocabulary. The cook's existing equipment and techniques provide the execution path.

---

### Fusion Night 1: Japanese-Cantonese Crossover

**Fan**: plain white jasmine rice

**Cai 1 -- Miso-glazed salmon (anchor, wok-finished)**
Salmon fillets sous vide at 125°F (Sunday bag), seared with kitchen torch, glazed with white miso + mirin + sake reduction brushed on at the end. The miso glaze is intensely salty-sweet -- 2-3 bites of fish per person with rice. Small platter.

**Cai 2 -- Garlic bok choy (wok, 3 minutes)**
Bok choy stir-fried fast with garlic, ginger, salt, splash of soy sauce. Straight from the Cantonese home playbook. Wok dish #2 in the sequence -- quick rinse after the salmon sear, bok choy in, done. Medium serving bowl.

**Cai 3 -- Soy-marinated eggs (batch-prepped Sunday, served cold)**
Soft-boiled eggs in soy-mirin-dashi marinade. Halved, room temp. Small plate. Already in the fridge from Sunday.

**Soup -- Miso-dashi from frozen cube**
Frozen miso-dashi-kombu concentrate cube dropped in boiling water. Fresh scallion and silken tofu floated. 5 minutes.

**Sunday prep load**: salmon vacuum sealed and frozen (sous vide reheat morning-of), soy eggs marinated, miso-dashi cubes frozen. **Active weeknight cooking**: torch the salmon (2 min), wok the bok choy (3 min), boil the soup (5 min), rice cooker runs itself.

### Fusion Night 2: Southern-Meets-Canton Rice Table

**Fan**: plain white jasmine rice

**Cai 1 -- Blackened catfish (anchor, cast iron)**
Catfish fillets (from frozen, thawed or sous vide) seared hard in cast iron with Cajun/Creole spice blend until blackened crust forms. The spice is aggressive -- a few bites per person drives a LOT of rice. Classic Southern technique, classic xiafan intensity. Small platter.

**Cai 2 -- Braised collard greens (batch-prepped Sunday, sous vide reheated)**
Collards braised with smoked meat, garlic, cider vinegar, and a pinch of sugar. Vacuum sealed Sunday, frozen, dropped in the sous vide bath that morning. The Southern green-vegetable tradition is already a batch-prep natural. Medium serving bowl.

**Cai 3 -- Quick pickled okra (batch-prepped Sunday, served cold)**
Okra blanched and pickled in vinegar-sugar-mustard seed brine. Keeps all week. Small bowl. Sharp, crunchy, cuts through the richness of the catfish and greens.

**Soup -- Gumbo base from frozen cube**
Frozen dark-roux gumbo concentrate cube in boiling water with a handful of fresh okra or greens. Rich, smoky, satisfying. 5 minutes.

**Sunday prep load**: collards braised and vacuum sealed, okra pickled, gumbo base cubes frozen. **Active weeknight cooking**: sear catfish (5 min), boil soup (5 min), rice cooker runs itself. Everything else is assembly.

---

### Fusion Night 3: Mediterranean Grain Table

**Fan**: farro (cooked in the rice cooker or on the stove, served in bowls like rice)

**Cai 1 -- Harissa-roasted lamb meatballs (anchor, oven)**
Ground lamb mixed with cumin, coriander, garlic, salt, formed into small meatballs, roasted at 450°F with a harissa glaze. Bold, spicy, aromatic. The meatballs are small -- 3-4 per person. Small platter or shallow bowl.

**Cai 2 -- White beans braised with preserved lemon (batch-prepped Sunday, sous vide reheated)**
Cannellini beans braised with preserved lemon, garlic, olive oil, and a touch of rosemary. Vacuum sealed Sunday, frozen, reheated from the bath. Creamy, savory, fills the vegetable/legume role. Medium bowl.

**Cai 3 -- Cucumber-yogurt-dill salad (made fresh, 5 minutes)**
Diced cucumber in thick Greek yogurt with dill, garlic, lemon juice, salt. Cool, creamy, acidic. Small bowl. The raita-equivalent for this table.

**Soup -- Tomato-harissa broth from frozen cube**
Concentrated tomato-harissa base cube in boiling water, finished with a drizzle of olive oil and torn fresh herbs. 5 minutes.

**Sunday prep load**: white beans braised and vacuum sealed, harissa-tomato soup base cubes frozen. Lamb meatballs can be formed Sunday and frozen raw on a sheet pan. **Active weeknight cooking**: roast meatballs (15 min at 450°F), assemble salad (5 min), boil soup (5 min), farro in the cooker.

### Fusion Night 4: Mexican-Canton Hybrid

**Fan**: plain white rice (or warm corn tortillas as a second fan option on the side)

**Cai 1 -- Chile-braised pork (anchor, batch-prepped or wok-finished)**
Pork shoulder cubes braised with dried chiles (guajillo, ancho), garlic, cumin, oregano, and a splash of cider vinegar. Can be batch-prepped Sunday and vacuum sealed, or braised fresh in a Dutch oven. Shredded or chunked, served in a medium bowl. Intensely savory, moderately spicy. Classic rice-killer.

**Cai 2 -- Elote-style corn salad (quick assembly)**
Charred corn kernels (from frozen, seared in cast iron) tossed with cotija, crema, lime juice, chili powder, and cilantro. Warm or room temp. Medium bowl. Crunchy, bright, sweet-salty.

**Cai 3 -- Nopalitos salad (batch-prepped Sunday, served cold or room temp)**
Cactus paddles (nopales) boiled or griddled until the slime cooks off, sliced into strips, tossed with lime juice, cilantro, diced onion, serrano, olive oil, and salt. A real dish -- served in a small bowl, taken with a spoon, eaten alongside rice and pork. Sharp, herbaceous, slightly mineral. The pickled-onions problem: they are a topping you scatter; nopalitos are something you serve and portion. Keeps 3+ days in the fridge.

**Soup -- Black bean soup from frozen cube**
Concentrated black bean soup base cube in boiling water, finished with a squeeze of lime and a spoon of crema. 5 minutes. Earthy, warming, inexpensive.

**Sunday prep load**: pork braised and vacuum sealed, pickled onions jarred, black bean soup cubes frozen. **Active weeknight cooking**: reheat pork from sous vide (or quick wok-toss to finish), char corn (5 min), boil soup (5 min), rice cooker runs itself.

### Fusion Night 5: Full Wok Sequence Night (Most Canton-Forward)

This is the night that leans hardest into the Kenji multi-dish wok workflow: one wok, 2-3 dishes in rapid sequence, all stir-fried. No oven, no sous vide reheating. Active cooking, but compressed into 15 minutes because everything is prepped.

**Fan**: plain white jasmine rice

**Cai 1 -- Mapo tofu (anchor, wok dish #1)**
Silken tofu in a spicy-numbing sauce with ground pork, doubanjiang, fermented black beans, Sichuan peppercorn, garlic, ginger. The most canonical rice-killer in the Chinese home repertoire. Wok dish first because it's sauciest -- keeps warm while the next two cook. One medium bowl.

**Cai 2 -- Dry-fried green beans (wok dish #2)**
Green beans blistered in the wok with garlic, dried shrimp, and soy sauce. Quick rinse of the wok between dishes. 3-4 minutes. Medium serving bowl.

**Cai 3 -- Stir-fried eggs with tomato (wok dish #3)**
Eggs scrambled loosely in the wok, tomato wedges added, seasoned with sugar, salt, and a splash of soy. A dish that exists in virtually every Chinese home kitchen. Quick, comforting, slightly sweet. Small bowl.

**Soup -- Seaweed and silken tofu gwuntong**
Dried seaweed (zicai/gim) added to boiling water, silken tofu cubes floated in, seasoned with salt and a few drops of sesame oil. Under 5 minutes. No egg -- the tomato-egg cai already has that covered.

**Sunday prep load**: minimal. Green beans washed and trimmed. Tofu drained. Pork portioned. Aromatics minced. This is a fresh-cook night. **Active weeknight cooking**: 15-minute wok sequence, 5-minute soup, rice cooker. The whole dinner is on the table in 20 minutes.

## What Changed From the Old Article Examples

The original `fan-cai-article.md` examples (lines 131-142) listed themed nights with dish names but no structural grounding:

- They implied a buffet of options rather than a decomposed mandatory meal
- They didn't specify which dish was the anchor vs vegetable vs sharp cai
- They didn't size anything to the cai-portion-guide
- They didn't connect to the cook's actual equipment (sous vide, wok, Sunday prep)
- They used "discovery" and "try something you wouldn't" language that belongs to banquet psychology

The Tier 3 nights above replace that approach. Each one:

- Names every cai with its role (louder/vegetable/sharp)
- Sizes to 6 people per the cai-portion-guide
- Specifies which components are Sunday batch-prepped, which are wok-sequenced, which are assembly
- Follows the table-service model: fan bowls, lazy susan, personal serving tools, mandatory full-table pass
- Treats every cai as part of one decomposed meal, not as elective discovery

The article's example section should be rewritten to use frames from this document (or simplified versions of them) once the format stabilizes.
