# Cai Portion Guide

Working guide for sizing cai in a fan/cai household so the table learns to rely on fan instead of gorging on cai.

## Core Reality

There is no single canonical Chinese household rule like "X grams of cai per person." What shows up instead are overlapping patterns:

- Grain-forward meal structure
- Vegetables next
- Meat/protein last
- Small household meals with modest dish counts
- Banquets and gatherings using many more dishes than a normal home meal

Do not copy banquet counts for weeknights. Use Chinese household logic, then deliberately bias it further toward fan.

## Starting Rule

For a normal weeknight of 6-8:

- 3 cai
- 1 light soup
- generous fan

One cai can be louder or more substantial.
One cai should usually be vegetable-dominant.
One cai should usually be small, sharp, or cold.

If guests are over or the night needs to feel fuller:

- 4 cai
- 1 light soup
- generous fan

Do not start at 6-8 cai unless you are doing an actual occasion spread or several of them are truly tiny cold dishes.

## The Barometers

Forget grams first. Use these.

### 1. Can one cai become someone's whole plate?

If yes, it is too big.

A cai should feel like:

- 2-4 bites per person for a meatier or louder dish
- 2-3 bites per person for a vegetable dish
- 1-2 bites per person for a pickle, egg, tofu, or cold side

For a 6-8 person table, each cai should look slightly modest when it lands.

Question to ask:

- Can one hungry person make a dinner out of this dish alone?

If the answer is yes, you made an entree, not cai.

### 2. The serving-vessel test

For 6-8 people:

- A meat-forward cai should usually fit on a small oval platter or one medium shallow bowl
- A leafy vegetable cai should fit in one medium serving bowl after cooking
- A pickle, egg, or cold tofu cai should fit in a small side bowl or appetizer plate
- Soup should fit in a small soup pot that gives everyone a modest bowl, not a vat

If you are regularly using your largest platter for a single cai, you are drifting back into Western-main territory.

### 3. The rice test

The fan should look abundant before anyone eats.

Not technically enough. Abundant.

Barometer:

- Everyone should be able to get a full first bowl immediately
- There should be enough visible reserve for at least half the table to take a second bowl
- If the rice cooker looks like "one bowl each and we're done," you undercooked the fan

For this house, think:

- 6 people: enough for 6 first bowls plus 3-4 extra bowls
- 8 people: enough for 8 first bowls plus 4-5 extra bowls

The point is psychological as much as caloric. Cai should look precious. Fan should look secure.

### 4. The refill test

What runs low first tells you what went wrong.

Good outcome:

- The first thing people need more of is rice

Bad outcomes:

- One meat cai vanishes immediately while rice sits untouched
- Everyone is picking protein pieces out and leaving peppers, onions, tofu, or vegetables behind
- People are building plates from cai instead of eating in rhythm with fan

If that happens, the usual fixes are:

- smaller meat pieces
- less pure protein volume
- more vegetable or tofu bulk in that cai
- a smaller serving platter
- more rice on the table from the start

## A Usable Weeknight Portion Model

### Model A: training the table

- 1 louder cai
- 1 vegetable cai
- 1 small sharp cai
- 1 light soup
- plenty of fan

Example:

- black bean pork with peppers
- garlic bok choy
- cucumber salad
- broth with greens
- rice

This is the best format for teaching reliance on fan.

### Model B: slightly fuller night

- 1 louder cai
- 1 medium cai
- 1 vegetable cai
- 1 small cold cai
- 1 light soup
- plenty of fan

Use this when guests are over or morale needs a lift.

### Model C: not fan/cai anymore

- two giant meat dishes
- one token vegetable
- heavy soup
- modest rice

This will produce gorging on cai every time.

## How to Train the Family to Rely on Fan

### Put the rice in bowls first

Do not let the table start pecking at cai while rice is still in the cooker.

The meal starts when bowls are in hands.

### Keep cai in smaller dishes

Large platters tell the body "this is the main event."
Smaller bowls tell the body "take some, then come back."

### Use awkward serving tools for meat cai

This is useful and slightly manipulative in a good way.

- For meat-heavy cai, use serving chopsticks or small tongs
- Avoid a big spoon that lets people shovel half a cup at once

You want people taking pieces, not scoops.

### Put the fan closest

On the lazy susan, rice should feel central and easy. Cai can orbit a little.

### Refill rice aggressively, cai selectively

If you are going back to the kitchen, the first refill should usually be fan.

## Cheat Sheet by Cai Type

### Meat-forward cai

Examples:

- black bean pork
- mapo tofu with some pork
- chile-braised chicken bits

Target:

- each person gets a few good bites, not a pile
- visually: enough to cover the center of a small platter, not heap over the edges

Red flag:

- people can mostly fill up on this alone

### Vegetable cai

Examples:

- bok choy
- cabbage
- green beans
- eggplant

Target:

- generous enough that people do not feel deprived
- still clearly a dish among dishes, not the whole meal

If you are going to overshoot, overshoot here.

### Sharp or cold cai

Examples:

- cucumber salad
- pickled radish
- soy eggs
- cold tofu

Target:

- small
- vivid
- appetite-opening
- not enough to become a plate by itself

Red flag:

- you made a deli-salad tub

### Soup

Target:

- light bowl each
- warming, clearing, stretching
- not chowder
- not stew
- not a second entree

## First Few Weeks Recommendation

Because this is new, do not optimize for authenticity first. Optimize for signal.

For the first 2-3 weeks:

- 3 cai max on regular nights
- 1 soup
- abundant fan
- one cai clearly louder than the others
- one cai explicitly small and sharp

Why:

- easier to prep
- easier to learn the family's behavior
- easier to spot when cai is oversized
- fewer leftovers

Once the family starts naturally eating bite of cai then mouthful of fan, widen the spread.

## Short Version

1. Each cai should look slightly too small to be dinner on its own.
2. The fan should look slightly too abundant.
3. If one cai disappears before people finish first bowls of fan, that cai was too large or too protein-heavy.
4. For 6-8 people on a normal night, aim for 3 cai plus soup, not banquet counts.
5. The first refill should usually be fan, not cai.

## Research Notes

- Hong Kong's Health Department `321` guidance frames meals as 3 parts grains, 2 parts vegetables, 1 part meat/fish/egg. Grain is primary, vegetables lead, meat is secondary.
- Ordinary Chinese home meal patterns cluster around small dish counts with rice taken for granted and multiple modest dishes rather than giant mains.
- Chinese gathering formulas often jump to `人数+2` style dish counts or 6-8 dishes for 6-8 people, but those are hosting formulas, not everyday family-meal targets.
