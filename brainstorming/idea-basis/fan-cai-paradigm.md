# Fan/Cai Paradigm

A framework for structuring meals around a staple grain (fan) and multiple small, intensely flavored shared dishes (cai), adapted for a large Western household.

## Core Concept

In Chinese culinary logic, a meal splits into two categories:
- **Fan** -- grains and staples. The caloric engine of the meal.
- **Cai** -- vegetables, meat, and everything else. Exists to provide salt, umami, and texture that makes you want to eat more fan.

The Western model treats protein as the main event and rice as a side. The fan/cai model inverts this: fan IS the meal, cai is the fuel that makes the engine taste good. The exact bite rhythm can vary by household -- separate bites, small amounts landed onto the bowl, different sequencing around the table -- but the structure stays the same: the cai exist to move fan, not to replace it.

## Why This Works for a Large Household

The Western "individual plate" model breaks down at 6-8 people. Cooking 8 steaks means half the table eats cold food. The fan/cai model scales effortlessly:

- **Adding guests**: throw two more scoops of rice in the cooker. Everyone takes a slightly smaller portion of cai and a larger portion of fan. The meal stays satisfying because flavor intensity doesn't dilute.
- **Temperature**: shared dishes served family-style from the center stay hotter longer than individual plated meals for 8.
- **Cost**: rice is the cheapest calorie. Cai can be small, intensely seasoned portions -- less protein needed per person when the fan is doing the heavy lifting.

## The Anchor Dish

The bridge between Western expectations and the fan/cai structure. One high-impact, freshly-cooked star dish per night that gives everyone a focal point.

- The anchor provides the psychological comfort of a "main" -- a recognizable protein or showpiece.
- The cai (2-3 smaller dishes) do the actual work of moving rice and providing variety.
- The anchor can come from any cuisine -- it's not restricted to Asian preparations.

This addresses "plate shock": the Western feeling that shared dishes mean less food. The anchor says "there IS a main" while the spread says "and there's more."

## Rice-Killer Logic

Xia fan -- literally "down with rice." Dishes that are so intensely seasoned (salty, spicy, fermented, umami-heavy) that they drive you to eat more rice to balance the palate.

- Traditional stir-fries are often "dry" or have a "clinging" sauce, not the thick cornstarch-heavy pours of Westernized Chinese food.
- Because the flavors are concentrated, you eat a higher ratio of rice to cai.
- Plain rice acts as a palate cleanser between bites, allowing much bolder flavors than a Western plate would tolerate.

## Table Service Logic

This is not banquet dining and not buffet psychology. The table holds one decomposed meal:

- **Every cai is part of dinner** -- the order can vary by seat position, but the dishes are not optional "extras."
- **Personal fan bowls are the home base** -- the bowl can stay mostly clean or receive small amounts from the shared dishes, but it should not become a one-pass Western mound.
- **Parallel access matters** -- the lazy susan solves reach; personal serving tools solve utensil bottlenecks.
- **Small first pass** -- everyone should be able to take some of each cai before loading up on seconds.
- **Small side plates are optional** -- useful for bones, drips, or staging, but not required for the model to work.

## Fan Rotation

Rice 75% of the time, with intentional variation to prevent fatigue:

- **Plain white rice** -- the default canvas
- **Japgokbap** -- Korean multigrain rice (barley, millet, black beans mixed in). Changes texture and color.
- **Half-milled or brown rice** -- occasional swap for variety
- **Farro or pearl barley** -- holds up well to saucy dishes, Mediterranean-fusion nights
- **Polenta** -- serves the exact same fan function for Italian/Mediterranean-leaning nights
- **Noodles (rice, soba, udon)** -- when the meal calls for it
- **Bread/flatbread** -- pitas, naan for Middle Eastern or Indian-fusion nights

The principle: the fan slot is always filled, but what fills it rotates to match the night's flavor direction.

## Soup as Daily Infrastructure

A light soup at every meal provides warmth, hydration, and stretches the spread.

- Making soup from scratch nightly is unsustainable. The solution: **frozen concentrated base cubes**.
- Freeze high-intensity bases in large ice cube trays (ginger-scallion-chicken, miso-dashi-kombu, tomato-harissa, coconut-lemongrass).
- At dinnertime: drop 2 cubes into boiling water, add fresh greens or mushrooms. Complex soup in 5 minutes.
- Different cubes match different cuisine directions -- the fusion model works here too.

## Sous Vide as Batch Component System

The sous vide machine shifts from "protein finisher" to "component assembly line":

- **Batch prep on Sunday**: vacuum seal cai components (braised daikon, glazed carrots, seasoned greens, slow-cooked pork belly, stewed beans). Freeze.
- **Weeknight reheat**: drop frozen bags into the bath in the morning. They're perfectly hot and held at temp when dinner comes.
- **The hold advantage**: 2-3 side components stay at serving temperature indefinitely while the anchor dish gets its final high-heat finish.
- **Result**: weeknight active cooking is reduced to one task -- the anchor. Everything else is assembly.

## The Weeknight Workflow

- **Morning**: drop 2 frozen component bags into sous vide bath
- **T-minus 20 min**: start rice/grain cooker
- **T-minus 10 min**: drop soup cubes into boiling water, add fresh herbs/greens
- **Final 5 min**: high-heat sear or stir-fry of the anchor dish
- **Table**: fan bowls filled, anchor and cai in shared dishes, soup in bowls, lazy susan turning, personal serving tools at each seat.

One active cooking task. Three-plus dishes on the table.

## Cultural Bridging

Strategies for transitioning a Western household without revolt:

- **Dedicated fan bowls**: everyone gets a real bowl for the staple. A small side plate can exist, but the bowl is the personal center of gravity.
- **Personal serving tools**: mini tongs, tweezers, or small spoons keep access parallel and stop the meal from turning into a queue.
- **Condiment gateway**: furikake, chili crisp, soy-marinated egg yolks, toasted sesame. Makes fan an active component people want to eat, not a passive filler.
- **The logistics argument**: "Cooking 8 individual portions means half of us eat cold food. Shared dishes mean everything stays hot." Frame it as efficiency, not cultural reenactment.
- **Gradual transition**: start with nights that already lean this direction, but teach the house rule early that the cai are one meal, not elective sides.

## The Leftover Lifecycle

Components and rice both transform across days:

- **Day 1**: fresh fluffy rice with dry stir-fry cai
- **Day 2**: yesterday's rice is now perfect for fried rice (dried grains separate cleanly)
- **Day 3**: last bits of rice become congee/jook
- **Cai components**: repurpose across nights. Braised pork from Monday becomes one cai component Wednesday. Soup base stretches across multiple dinners.

## Microcultural Fusion

The fan/cai structure is a framework, not a cuisine. It accepts any flavor tradition:

- A Mediterranean night: polenta (fan) + harissa-roasted lamb (anchor) + marinated artichokes + olive-caper relish (cai) + tomato broth (soup)
- A Japanese night: rice (fan) + teriyaki salmon (anchor) + spinach ohitashi + pickled daikon (cai) + miso soup
- A Southern/Creole night: rice (fan) + blackened catfish (anchor) + collard greens + black-eyed peas (cai) + gumbo base soup
- A Mexican-fusion night: rice or tortillas (fan) + carne asada (anchor) + elote salad + pickled onions + refried beans (cai) + tortilla soup

The structure stays constant. The flavors rotate freely.

## Sunday Batch Prep

The keystone habit that makes the weeknight workflow possible:

- Cook 4-6 cai components, vacuum seal, freeze
- Prepare 3-4 soup base concentrates, freeze in cube trays
- Pre-season and bag any proteins for the week
- Prep condiments and pickles that last the week
- Make any sauces or marinades

Sunday prep is an investment of 2-3 hours that buys 4-5 nights of minimal-effort dinners.
