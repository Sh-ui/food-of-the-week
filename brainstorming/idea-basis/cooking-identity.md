# Cooking Identity

Synthesized profile of this household's cooking style, preferences, and instincts. This is a living document -- update after each planning session with new learnings.

## Household

- 6 core people eating every dinner, with 1-2 additional guests rotating on a semi-regular basis. Plan for 6-8.
- Primary cook is technically skilled, opinionated, creative. Thinks like a chef, not a recipe follower.
- Helpers: Dad (chopping, prep), sister (rice cooker, simple tasks, pressing tortillas).
- Every meal must feel like a real, complete dinner for the full table. If it wouldn't satisfy everyone, it's not a meal.

## How This Cook Thinks

- Recipes are sketches, not procedures. Ratios and flavor instincts matter more than exact measurements.
- Meals emerge from intersections of ingredients and ideas, not strict tradition. A "deconstructed Midwestern broiler steak dinner" is a natural concept here.
- Three movers in every dish: a flavor base (aromatics, fats), a structural ingredient (protein, starch, veg), and a finishing accent (acid, herb, crunch).
- The week should have variety in pattern and cuisine -- never two similar meals back-to-back.

## Meal Structure: Fan/Cai Framework

The household is transitioning from a Western "one big dish per plate" model to a fan/cai-inspired structure. See `brainstorming/idea-basis/fan-cai-paradigm.md` for the full framework.

- **Fan** (staple grain) is the core of the meal -- rice 75% of the time, rotated with farro, polenta, noodles, or bread to prevent fatigue.
- **Cai** (small, intensely flavored shared dishes) served family-style from the center of the table on a lazy susan. 2-3 per night.
- **Anchor dish** -- one of the cai dishes, will be a high-impact, freshly-cooked star each night. This bridges Western expectations. Can be any cuisine.
- **Soup** -- a light soup at most (but not all) meals, built from frozen concentrated base cubes for speed.
- **Service style**: individual fan bowls as the personal home base, soup bowls/spoons, and personal serving tools so everyone can access shared cai in parallel. A small side plate is optional for bones, drips, or temporary staging, but not structurally required.
- **Table logic**: this is not banquet grazing. The cai together make one decomposed meal, and everyone should make a first pass across the full set rather than building a single overloaded private plate.

The anchor dish is where the cook's existing patterns (sheet pan, skillet braise, stir-fry, etc.) live. Cai components are smaller-scale: quick pickles, blanched greens, dry stir-fries, braised sides, condiment builds.

## Sunday Batch Prep

The keystone that makes the weeknight workflow sustainable:

- Vacuum seal and freeze 4-6 cai components (braised vegetables, seasoned greens, stewed beans, slow-cooked meats)
- Prepare 3-4 soup base concentrates, freeze in large ice cube trays
- Pre-season and bag proteins for the week
- Prep condiments and pickles that last the week
- Make sauces and marinades

Weeknight workflow becomes: morning sous vide reheat of frozen cai bags, rice cooker on, soup cubes in boiling water, then one active cooking task -- the anchor.

## Cuisine Range

This cook draws freely from multiple traditions and mixes them without anxiety. The fan/cai framework is cuisine-agnostic -- it accepts flavors from any tradition (microcultural fusion):
- **Chinese**: wok hei, dry stir-fries, mapo tofu, black bean sauce, XO, chili crisp, congee, rice-killer logic
- **Japanese**: curry cubes, mirin, sake, furikake, sesame, rice wine vinegar, Japanese dressing, sticky rice, katsu, panko, ohitashi, dashi
- **Korean**: japgokbap (multigrain rice), gochujang, banchan mentality (small shared sides), kimchi
- **Southeast Asian**: coconut milk, lemongrass, fish sauce, Thai basil, sambal, peanut sauces
- **French**: white wine, tarragon, crème fraîche, Dijon, bonne femme, fond-based sauces, poaching
- **Mediterranean/Middle East**: harissa, couscous, preserved lemon, olives, chickpeas, feta, yogurt sauces
- **Mexican**: masa harina (multiple colors), refried beans, corn tortillas, cotija, pickled onions, crema
- **Italian**: puttanesca, orzo, San Marzano tomatoes, Parmesan, anchovies
- **Southern/Creole**: roux-based gumbos, black-eyed peas, collard greens, coconut milk curries with Creole spice
- **British comfort**: roasts, gravies, popovers/yorkshires, peas, mash
- **American**: deconstructed comfort food, chili, sheet-pan dinners, power salads

Any given week might include a Japanese night alongside a Tuscan braise alongside Mexican tostadas. The fan/cai structure stays constant -- the flavors rotate freely.

## Equipment and Techniques

- **Wok** is the primary multi-dish weeknight tool. One wok can sequence 2-3 cai in rapid succession (stir-fry, quick rinse, next dish). Home burner BTUs are fine -- this is not restaurant wok hei, it's component assembly. The shape lets you toss small batches fast.
- **Sous vide** is core infrastructure on two levels. For proteins: circulator with remote app, family drops sealed bags in the bath, chef finishes when home, bag juices become sauces and gravies. For the fan/cai workflow: batch-prepped cai components are frozen in vacuum bags and reheated from frozen in the sous vide bath, held at serving temperature until dinner. This turns weeknight cooking into assembly.
- **Vacuum sealer** for batch-prepping cai components and proteins. Seal, freeze, reheat from frozen in the sous vide bath. The sealer is what connects Sunday prep to weeknight assembly.
- **Kitchen torch** for searing sous vide proteins (salmon, chicken, steak).
- **Rice cooker** for rice, multigrain rice, couscous. In the fan/cai model this runs almost every night. Can double as a steamer (pork patty or fish steamed over the rice).
- **Steamer** (bamboo or metal, over wok or standalone) for steamed fish, steamed egg custard, steamed pork patties. Cantonese home cooking leans heavily on steaming as a no-wok-needed technique.
- **Lazy susan** for center-table family-style service.
- **Personal serving tools** (mini tongs, tweezers, small spoons) so access to the shared dishes is parallel instead of turn-based.
- **Large ice cube trays** for freezing concentrated soup bases.
- **Convection oven** for high-heat finishing (500°F roasts, sheet pans, meatballs).
- **Cast iron and heavy skillets** for searing and fond-building.
- Loves high-heat finishing: charring cabbage wedges, searing proteins in ripping hot pans, building fond then deglazing.
- Vegetables pre-cooked sous vide (beets, sweet potatoes, green beans, mirepoix, braised daikon, glazed carrots).

## Meal Patterns

### Anchor Patterns (the louder cai each night)

These are the cook's modular patterns for the one cai that reads as the star:
- **Wok stir-fry**: fast, high heat, minimal chopping. Natural anchor for rice nights. Can sequence 2-3 cai in one session.
- **Steamed dish**: fish slices, pork patty with preserved vegetable, whole fish. The steamer or rice cooker does the work -- no active cooking. Core Cantonese home technique.
- **Skillet/cast iron sear**: blackened fish, seared steak, charred protein. High-heat finish, aggressive seasoning, few bites per person.
- **Oven roast**: meatballs, sheet pan protein + veg at high heat (450-500°F). Minimal mess, scalable.
- **Braise/stew**: braised tofu with pork, chile-braised pork, bazella b'lahme. Can be the anchor on nights where it's freshly made, or a batch-prepped cai on nights where something else leads.
- **One-pot exception**: pasta, noodle soups, chili. Everything in one vessel. On one-pot nights the fan/cai structure relaxes -- the pot IS the meal.
- **Family-style assembly exception**: tacos, pitas, and similar build-your-own meals are a separate pattern, not the same as fan/cai. Use when appropriate, but don't let them redefine the shared mandatory-cai table logic.

### Cai Patterns (the supporting cast)

Small-scale dishes that fill out the table. Most are batch-prepped Sunday and reheated:
- **Quick pickles and ferments**: pickled onions, daikon, cucumbers, okra. Last the week. The small/sharp/cold cai slot.
- **Blanched/dressed greens**: spinach ohitashi/gomaae, garlic bok choy, sesame green beans, blanched choy sum with oyster sauce. Sous vide reheat well. The vegetable cai slot.
- **Dry stir-fries**: intensely seasoned small portions. Rice-killers. Green beans, eggplant, shredded pork.
- **Braised sides**: daikon, carrots, white beans, collard greens, root vegetables. Freeze and reheat perfectly.
- **Steamed dishes**: steamed egg custard, steamed tofu, steamed fish. Gentle, soft, palate-resetting.
- **Tofu preparations**: braised tofu with minced pork, mapo tofu, cold silken tofu with soy-ginger.
- **Egg dishes**: soy-marinated eggs, steamed egg custard, stir-fried eggs with tomato. Batch-prepped or quick-cooked. Eggs can be a legitimate cai component -- they just cannot solo-anchor a meal.
- **Condiment builds**: chili crisp, furikake, flavored salts, compound butters, yogurt sauces, raita, pickled chili.

## The F-Word Ethos

The overarching philosophy comes from Gordon Ramsay's "The F Word": restaurant-style meals that anyone can make at home.
- Take normal food, keep its soul, give it one or two touches that make it feel deliberate and composed.
- If the main component is soft, add sharpness and crunch. If humble, add something clean and elevated.
- The garnish or finishing sauce is what makes it "restaurant" -- the base stays homey.

## Protein and Sourcing

- Proteins often come from ButcherBox deliveries -- frozen, portioned, vacuum sealed.
- Common proteins across weeks: salmon, tilapia, cod, chicken (breasts and thighs), ground beef, ground pork, ground lamb, pork chops, pork tenderloin, flat iron steak, beef tenderloin, Italian sausage, scallops, shrimp.
- Frozen-to-pan or rapid-thaw methods are normal and expected.
- Thaw time should be factored into the week's sequence.

## Budget and Grocery Approach

- Budget varies week to week. Some weeks have full ButcherBox runs; some weeks are near-zero grocery trips.
- When the cook says money is tight: default to using what's on hand. Grocery trips should be surgical strikes -- the minimum items that unlock the most meals.
- Stretch ingredients (potatoes, ground beef, cabbage, dried beans) are valued because they transform across multiple dishes without feeling repetitive.
- Don't pad the grocery list with nice-to-haves unless the cook asks.

## Energy and Motivation

- The cook's energy varies week to week. Some weeks are high-ambition; some are survival mode.
- Match the plan's ambition to the cook's stated energy and situation.
- Structure helps with low periods but pressure doesn't. Gentle momentum beats ambitious overcommitment.
- When energy is low, lean toward bought convenience (pitas over homemade flatbread, canned beans over dried-from-scratch) without judgment.

## Ingredient Instincts

- **Eggs** can be cai (soy eggs, steamed custard, tomato-egg stir-fry) but cannot solo-anchor a meal's protein. Also used as binders, in batters, in fried rice.
- **Beans and lentils** can anchor a meatless meal if the dish has enough body and character. Plain beans + rice alone is not enough.
- **Fridge garnishes** do heavy lifting: pickled red onions, cherry pepper relish, greek yogurt as crema, feta, Parmesan, specialty oils and vinegars. They turn a simple table into a complete spread across multiple nights.
- **Specialty oils and vinegars** (jalapeño olive oil, white pineapple balsamic) should be featured intentionally, not wasted as background.
- The Asian pantry (miso, oyster sauce, fish sauce, sesame oil, furikake, rice wine vinegar, MSG) is always stocked and should be used when the meal calls for it.

## Smart Leftovers

Meals are better when leftovers transform into a different meal identity rather than just being reheated:
- Roast protein one night → sandwiches or composed plates the next
- Big-batch stew or chili → different vehicle the next day (with rice, on tostadas, in bowls)
- Cooked beans → side one night, folded into a different dish later

The fan/cai model adds its own lifecycle:
- Fresh rice → day-old fried rice → congee from the last bits
- Cai components repurpose across nights (braised pork Monday becomes one cai component Wednesday)
- Soup bases stretch across multiple dinners with different fresh additions each time
